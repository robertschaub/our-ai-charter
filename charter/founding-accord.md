# Our AI Charter — the Founding Accord
### A public charter for trustworthy AI — built together, answerable to people.

_Draft v0.5 (2026-06-15). Open for comment._

---

**Preamble**

Artificial intelligence has become critical infrastructure for how the world learns, decides, and creates. Too much of it now depends on a few providers, in a few jurisdictions, whose offerings can be withdrawn, constrained, or redirected by forces no user controls. The answer is not retreat into rival camps, but cooperation — a shared, trustworthy foundation for AI that is open, plural, secure, and answerable to people. A network, not a fortress.

We commit to building that foundation together, and to being held to the values and commitments below.

**Our values**

- **Humanity** — we serve people and the common good first.
- **Justice** — power over AI must be accountable and fairly shared.
- **Respect** — we protect autonomy, privacy, consent, and the plurality of the world's peoples and views.
- **Truth** — we are honest, transparent, and verifiable, and we stand against manipulation.
- **Solidarity** — we build together, support one another, and steward the shared commons.

**Three ways to take part**

- **Supporter** — any person or institution may publicly endorse this Accord.
- **Member** — organisations that join the Charter, accept its shared, capture-resistant governance, and commit to the path below.
- **Pilot participant** — organisations may volunteer a specific deployed system for a draft evaluation report. No Mark or certificate is issued in this phase.
- **Future Trust Mark candidate / holder** — once a conformity scheme exists, members may submit a specific model or system for independent audit against the commitments. Any future Mark is granted **per model and version — never as a blanket badge for an organisation** — and may be revoked.

**Key term**

**"Material"** means significant enough that a reasonable user, buyer, regulator, assessor, or affected person would weigh it when deciding whether to trust, procure, oversee, or challenge the system. Examples include model-family changes, critical provider or data-source changes, external orders or pressure to restrict or shut down the system, and AI use in a consequential decision.

**What people need to be able to demand**

People who use, buy, study, regulate, or are affected by AI need more than promises. They need to know:

- **What is this system for?** The intended uses, prohibited uses, assessed version, known limits, and risk context.
- **Who controls it?** The operator, material dependencies, and any power to update, restrict, recall, or shut it down, including external orders or pressure to use those powers.
- **Who is accountable?** The entity that answers when the system is deployed, procured, or embedded in a public workflow.
- **How can people challenge it?** The routes for objection, review, correction, reversal, withdrawal, or escalation where harm, consequential decisions, restrictions, or shutdowns are involved.
- **Who can inspect it?** The boundary between public transparency, confidential audit, lawful oversight, and protected research access.
- **Can its claims and failures be checked?** The evidence trail for factual claims, consequential decisions, restriction decisions, incidents, material changes, corrections, withdrawals, and exit paths.

Those needs are the bridge from the manifesto's three obligations to concrete commitments.

**Our commitments** (to guide current pilot evaluation, and to earn and keep any future Trust Mark for a given model or system under independent audit):

1. **Purpose and scope.** We state the assessed system, version, intended uses, prohibited uses, known limits, and risk tier before claiming alignment with the Charter.
2. **Control and dependencies.** We publish and maintain a clear map of the operator, accountable deployment owner, material providers, jurisdictions, critical dependencies, and powers to update, restrict, recall, or shut the system down. The basis and use of those powers — including material external orders, coercive pressure, or emergency demands where disclosure is lawful — are documented, scoped, time-limited where possible, and logged for public, assessor, or lawful oversight review.
3. **Accountability.** We name the entity that answers for the deployed system, the public claims made about it, the support route, the relevant jurisdiction, and the records retained to substantiate those claims.
4. **Challenge and remedy.** We provide a working complaint and appeal route, with notice where AI is materially involved, published response times, human review for consequential uses, and privacy-preserving outcome records. Where appropriate, review can lead to correction, reversal, withdrawal, escalation, or a lawful challenge to a restriction or shutdown decision.
5. **Inspection access.** We define what is public, what independent assessors can inspect confidentially, what lawful oversight bodies can require, and what protected researchers may access where a legal, contractual, or approved research basis exists, under privacy and security safeguards.
6. **Evidence, failures, and correction.** For factual use-cases and consequential decisions, we make sources, evidence, limits, and uncertainty viewable; maintain correction records, incident records, restriction and withdrawal records, material-change logs, reassessments, and documented exit paths; and let independent assessors test whether cited sources or documented evidence support the claims made. Unsupported marketing or capability claims are a breach.

This is the protection against authoritarian switch-off orders or manipulation: not a fantasy that private systems can ignore every lawful state act, but a requirement that material control interventions are attributable, proportionate, evidenced, logged, reviewable, and paired with continuity or exit planning when public reliance is material. Secret, unilateral, unreviewable control is not compatible with the Charter.

Because state regulation is not uniform across jurisdictions, this Accord cannot simply sit above one shared legal floor. A future **common certification baseline** would be the minimum set of operational commitments any system carrying the Charter mark must meet, regardless of jurisdiction. Stricter local law prevails; weaker or silent local law does not lower the baseline. Higher tiers or modules can add stronger commitments beyond the baseline.

**Independent and fair by design**

If a conformity scheme is created, audits should be conducted by accredited, independent assessors — not chosen or paid directly by the audited party — against a published standard. A **subsidised and peer-verification tier** should keep any future Mark within reach of small, open-source, and Global-South builders, so trust is never a privilege of the well-funded.

**How this binds us**

These are commitments to be checked, not admired. In the current phase, they guide draft evaluation and public critique. In any future conformity scheme, they would create a common baseline for the Charter, but they would not certify legal compliance in every jurisdiction. If a Trust Mark is created, it is earned through independent audit and may be withdrawn. Values with teeth — or they are only words.

**An open invitation**

This Accord belongs to no single company or country. Any trustworthy organisation — anywhere, subject to law and to these commitments — that shares these values is invited to endorse it, help build the governance, or volunteer a system for pilot evaluation. Independent audit and any Mark come only if a conformity scheme is created. Individuals and institutions may endorse it as supporters.

## Open decisions (next phase)

- **Baseline and higher tiers:** decide which commitments belong in the common baseline and which become stronger optional modules.
- **Governance pack** (the credibility layer): funding caps + conflict-of-interest policy; independent board/assembly with Global-South & civil-society seats *by rule*; auditor accreditation; appeals/remediation; public audit + revocation history.
- **Legal floor, certification baseline, higher tiers:** define how the Accord relates to uneven state regulation, procurement rules, notified-body regimes, and buyer requirements without implying legal compliance.
- **Coercive-order handling:** define how systems document, disclose, challenge, and recover from government, platform, provider, or other external orders to restrict, manipulate, recall, or shut down a system, including what can be public and what must be held for confidential oversight.
- **Security assurance model:** threat model; audit scope (weights/APIs/data/agents/infra/supply chain); audit frequency + continuous monitoring; incident-reporting timelines; transparency logs for signatures/provenance; defence against audit-shopping & "provenance theater" (signing ≠ lawful/consensual data).
- **Go-to-market:** buyers-first (procurement pledges) + a narrow testable v1 (one standard, one governance model, one audit method, one public report index, 3–5 diverse regional pilots; publish results and withdrawals early).
- **Positioning:** explicitly complement/extend the Digital Public Goods Alliance's registry model and the Coalition for Secure AI's security standards rather than duplicate them.
