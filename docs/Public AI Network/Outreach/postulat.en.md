# Postulate (Draft): Trustworthy, Internationally Anchored Public-AI Infrastructure — Switzerland's Contribution and Governance Framework

**Instrument:** Postulate (instructs the Federal Council to examine a matter and report back; adoption by one chamber suffices)
**To be submitted by:** [sponsoring member of parliament — e.g. G. Andrey (Greens), K. Christ (Green Liberal Party / GLP), Ch. Juillard (The Centre / Die Mitte), or M. Michel (FDP.The Liberals)]
**Chamber:** [National Council / Council of States]
**Date:** [dd.mm.yyyy]

## Submitted text

The Federal Council is instructed to examine, in a report, how Switzerland — building on existing strengths (Swiss AI Initiative / Swiss National AI Institute, Apertus, CSCS) and on its role as a partner country of the Paris Charter of Current AI — can contribute, as a credible host and central hub, to an internationally supported and governed public-AI infrastructure.

The report should set out which models of institutional stewardship and governance would create a framework that, among other things, meets the following requirements:

1. Ensuring traceable provenance of data, models, and funds, together with transparent assumptions and verifiable, reproducible methods;
2. Transparent funding — for example, majority-public or non-profit — in which commercial contributions are disclosed but exercise no controlling function;
3. Effective protection against political and commercial capture, through independent oversight outside the executive, publicly accessible audit reports, and clearly defined responsibilities;
4. An internationally open and multilingual orientation that also connects to the AI Summit 2027 in Geneva.

In addition, the report should set out possible fields of action for the Confederation as well as concrete next steps. These should be aligned in substance with the thrust of Motion 24.3209 ("For a sovereign digital infrastructure in Switzerland in the age of artificial intelligence").

## Rationale

The base infrastructure of artificial intelligence — models, computing power, and data — is increasingly concentrating among a few commercial and geopolitical actors. For a democratic state under the rule of law, this carries a double risk: on the one hand, a dependence on opaque systems that could be switched off at any time; on the other, an erosion of democratic judgement when the provenance of the data, the underlying assumptions, and the methods of these systems are not publicly verifiable.

Switzerland already has robust foundations to counter this development: with Apertus, there is a fully open, publicly funded language model (EPFL, ETH Zurich, CSCS; open weights, data, and methods). The Swiss AI Initiative (SNAI) brings together the country's scientific expertise, and Switzerland is already a partner country of the international Current AI initiative. Moreover, the AI Summit 2027 in Geneva offers a concrete window in which Switzerland can take on a credible role as host and bridge-builder.

The Confederation is currently preparing draft legislation on AI regulation (among other things, to implement the Council of Europe Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence), and Motion 24.3209 already addresses sovereign digital infrastructure (e.g. cloud services). What is still missing, however, is a clear *governance for an open, public-interest AI infrastructure*: how can it be operated in a way that is trustworthy, able to connect internationally, and protected against capture? Trust does not arise from a "Made in Switzerland" label alone, but from traceable provenance, transparent assumptions, verifiable methods, and clear responsibilities.

This postulate calls on the Federal Council to analyse precisely this need for action and to set out corresponding models — as a sensible complement to the work already underway, without prejudging it.
