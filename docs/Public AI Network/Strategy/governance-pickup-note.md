# Public AI Infrastructure Governance - Pickup Note

_Working note, 2026-06-18. Purpose: preserve the core conclusion from a public discussion about an open Swiss AI model and make it usable for the next drafting pass._

## Context

The trigger was a public discussion about whether Switzerland should invest heavily in an open national AI model. The durable point is broader than any single model, country, or project name.

The Charter-relevant question is:

> How can open AI be organised so that free societies can understand, inspect, shape, and contest the infrastructure they increasingly rely on?

## Core conclusion

The Charter should not frame public-interest AI mainly as a national technology asset. A national effort can be valuable, but the larger opportunity is an internationally governable public AI infrastructure: open enough for inspection, shared enough for legitimacy, and protected enough to resist capture.

This implies a clear order of goals:

1. **International democratic resilience — the core mission.** Protect and strengthen democracy and free, rule-of-law values in the digital space — internationally, and thereby also in Switzerland — by making AI infrastructure more inspectable, contestable, and answerable to public-interest governance. This is the shared motivation behind both *Our AI Charter* and FactHarbor, pursued at international, European, and Swiss levels in parallel and starting operationally in Switzerland as the entry point.
2. **Swiss and local innovation capacity — a complementary benefit.** Durable Swiss expertise, tools, teams, and institutions — rather than merely buying finished AI services from dominant providers — is a benefit the initiative actively embraces and a strong reason for national governments to invest. It follows from the first goal rather than driving it.

## Argument

1. **The issue is infrastructure, not one model.** AI is becoming part of how people learn, decide, govern, trade, and form public opinion. The key question is who understands and shapes that infrastructure, not which single model wins.
2. **Open does not automatically mean trustworthy.** Open weights, open code, or a national label do not by themselves create trust. Trust requires inspectable provenance, stated assumptions, tested limits, named responsibilities, correction routes, and governance that can be challenged.
3. **National framing can limit international relevance.** If a project is framed too tightly as "a Swiss model" or around one national flagship, other states, universities, companies, and public institutions may see themselves as users or guests rather than co-stewards.
4. **The economic value is capability spillover.** The macroeconomic benefit is not only the output model. It is the development of methods, tooling, data and evaluation competence, safety practice, governance practice, and talent that later move into universities, companies, public administration, and startups.
5. **International co-stewardship multiplies the value.** Local capability grows more strongly when it is connected to global open-source architectures, shared standards, reciprocal evaluation, and partners that can co-govern rather than merely consume.

## Governance proposal

The open question is not first "Should a new foundation be created?" The better first question is:

> Which stewardship structure can keep a public AI effort internationally open, transparently financed, scientifically credible, and protected against political, national, institutional, or commercial capture?

Possible forms include an international foundation, an alliance, a neutral host inside an existing institution, or an expanded mandate for existing structures. The legal form is secondary to the governance requirements.

A credible structure should:

- let partners co-shape the agenda rather than merely adopt a national model;
- publish decision rules, funding sources, conflict-of-interest rules, and capture safeguards;
- separate technical work, standard-setting, evaluation, and funding influence where possible;
- maintain public evidence about provenance, methods, limitations, releases, incidents, and correction;
- support scientific challenge, public-interest oversight, and withdrawal or narrowing of claims when evidence is insufficient;
- stay compatible with adjacent initiatives instead of duplicating them.

## Relationship to existing Charter documents

This note reinforces existing repository positions:

- [Founding Accord](https://github.com/robertschaub/our-ai-charter/blob/main/docs/AI%20Assurance%20and%20Certification/Framework/founding-accord.md): "a network, not a fortress"; capture-resistant governance; people need to know who controls, inspects, and is accountable for AI systems.

- [Landscape and positioning](https://github.com/robertschaub/our-ai-charter/blob/main/docs/AI%20Assurance%20and%20Certification/Background/landscape-and-positioning.md): author the standard, federate execution, and avoid a new silo.
- [Risk and vulnerability audit](https://github.com/robertschaub/our-ai-charter/blob/main/docs/AI%20Assurance%20and%20Certification/Background/risk-and-vulnerability-audit.md): concentrated control, coercive restriction, evidence theatre, and resource/global-access asymmetry are core risks.

## Candidate adjacent initiatives to verify

Future drafting or outreach should verify current status before citing:

- Swiss and European open-model efforts: Apertus, Swiss AI Initiative, SNAI, OpenEuroLLM.
- Public-interest and open-evaluation efforts: Public AI, Public AI Network, MLCommons, Current AI, AI Alliance.
- Regional open-model projects: SEA-LION, OLMo, Latam-GPT, Sarvam, Lelapa, and related initiatives.
- Public digital infrastructure bodies: Digital Public Goods Alliance, Linux Foundation, OASIS, CEN, and other neutral hosts.

The drafting point is not to list every project. It is to show that a national contribution should be designed as a node in a broader public AI ecosystem.

## Follow-up for next drafting pass

1. Keep this as a background note. The generic co-stewardship and capture-resistance principle has been reflected in the Founding Accord and certification model working drafts.
2. If adding further normative text, keep it general: avoid centering one country or one project; state the governance principle, not a preferred legal vehicle.
3. Source-check the named initiatives before using them in public-facing claims.
4. If a future clause needs to restate the principle, keep it close to this:

   > Public AI infrastructure should be internationally co-stewarded where possible. National or regional efforts can contribute strong nodes when governance lets partners inspect, challenge, and help shape the infrastructure without becoming dependent on a single state, company, funder, host institution, or audited party. Funding support should be disclosed, capped, and conflict-managed.

5. Preserve the phase discipline: this repository can propose governance requirements and pilot methods; it does not yet create, certify, or operate a public AI infrastructure body.
