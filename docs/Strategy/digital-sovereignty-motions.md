# Swiss digital-sovereignty motions (24.3209 + 26.3221) — engagement note

Two motions on digital sovereignty have been adopted by the Council of States and are now before the National Council. They form a pair: both express a forming, cross-party (Die-Mitte–led) mandate for sovereign, open AI; both fund the **building** of capability; and neither defines how publicly-funded AI is kept inspectable, accountable, and capture-resistant. That governance/trust layer is what this workstream offers — so the motions are a window, not a competitor.

Full record and sources: [Verified Findings → Live political window](../Evidence/verified-findings.md#live-political-window).

## Motion 24.3209 (Juillard) — the structural / legislative anchor

**Motion 24.3209** "Für eine souveräne digitale Infrastruktur in der Schweiz im Zeitalter der künstlichen Intelligenz" — Charles Juillard (Die Mitte / Jura). It asks the Federal Council to present a **law revision** (*Gesetzesrevision*) so the Confederation — together with the private sector, the cantons, and research institutes — can **drive forward, co-finance, and supervise** the build-up of a sovereign digital infrastructure, including a cloud service and an independent exchange platform. **Federal Council recommended rejection (22 May 2024); adopted by the Council of States 31:11 (March 2026); now before the National Council committee** (status June 2026; no plenary vote in the summer session).

**Role here — the structural anchor.** It is the substantive peg for the [Postulat](../Outreach/postulat.en.md), whose submitted text aligns with this motion's thrust, and its eventual *law revision* (and any consultation / Vernehmlassung) is the concrete window for injecting independent-governance criteria. Note the gap the Charter answers: 24.3209 places supervision *inside* the executive ("überwachen"), whereas trustworthy oversight needs to sit *outside* it — raised as a complement, not a criticism. Juillard (Die Mitte, Council of States) is a natural carrier / ally.

## Motion 26.3221 (Z'graggen) — the funding / pilot vehicle

**Motion 26.3221** "Impulsprogramm zur Stärkung der digitalen Souveränität der Schweiz" — Heidi Z'graggen (Die Mitte / Uri). A **time-limited, targeted seed-funding** programme (*Anschubfinanzierung*) for pilot projects across four fields — **digital infrastructure, open-source technologies, cybersecurity, and artificial intelligence** — meant to activate existing state, industry, and science competencies, and framed explicitly as *not* an expansion of the state (no permanent subsidies). **Federal Council recommended rejection** (sovereignty "already covered" via *Digitale Schweiz 2026* / *Digitale Bundesverwaltung*, plus budget limits); **adopted by the Council of States 30:7 (1 abstention) on 10 June 2026; now before the National Council.** A motion binds the Federal Council only once *both* chambers adopt it.

**Role here — the funding / pilot vehicle.** The AI / open-source field is the natural docking point for a public-interest governance + evaluation pilot. The access route that matters is the eventual Federal Council implementation — lead office, budget, duration, and **how pilots are proposed and selected**. Z'graggen (Die Mitte, Council of States) is a natural carrier / ally.

## Where *Our AI Charter* / the Public AI Network could plug in

1. **The governance/trust complement.** Offer the Charter's five public obligations ([Charter Commitments](../AI%20Assurance%20and%20Certification/Framework/charter-commitments.md)) and the *[Grounding-Faithfulness & Contestability](../AI%20Assurance%20and%20Certification/Protocol/grounding-faithfulness-and-contestability.md)* evaluation module as the trust layer for any AI these motions fund — additive to, not competing with, Apertus / SNAI.
2. **A candidate pilot.** A public-interest **governance + evaluation pilot** — e.g. an openness/evidence card plus a trial evaluation of an Apertus-class or other open model — is the kind of "innovative pilot project" 26.3221's AI / open-source field describes, and fills the gap both motions leave open.
3. **Open source done credibly.** 26.3221 elevates open source as sovereignty policy; the Charter supplies the missing distinction — *open weights are necessary but not sufficient; public AI also needs evidence, accountability, and correction* — so that publicly funded AI becomes something people can rely on and hold to account: claims they can weigh before acting on them, data provenance and methods open to scrutiny, and control that is reversible and outside any single actor's hands — and can translate "open" into auditable public-interest criteria for funded pilots.
4. **A timing signal.** Two Council-of-States-adopted motions show a forming Swiss mandate for sovereign, open, public AI — usable evidence in orientation conversations, the [Non-Paper](../Outreach/non-paper.en.md), the [Swiss AI Action-Plan contribution](swiss-action-plan-contribution.md), and a Geneva 2027 contribution.
5. **A formal thread later — via a carrier, not first.** Consistent with this workstream's *alliance/mandate first, lawmaking later* sequence ([initiation strategy](initiation-strategy.md)): once a coalition and a concrete deliverable exist, a carrier council member could file an interpellation/postulate on *how* publicly-funded AI will be governed and evaluated. A non-parliamentarian engages this thread only indirectly, via a council member — never as a first move.

## Honest constraints

- **Nothing to apply to yet.** Neither motion is finally adopted (each needs the National Council, then Federal Council implementation). For 26.3221 no budget, duration, lead office, or pilot-selection mechanism exists; for 24.3209 the law revision is not drafted. Today this is positioning, not a live funding call.
- **Intent ≠ fact.** Do not imply any coalition, endorsement, or that the Charter is a selected pilot.
- **Additive, not competitive** with SNAI / Apertus or the motions' backers; disclose any commercial ties.
