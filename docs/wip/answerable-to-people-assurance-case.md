# Assurance case — *Answerable to people* (obligation 2)

*A Claims → Arguments → Evidence view of one obligation: the **claim** (the public promise), the **duties** that argue it, the **modules** that evidence it, and the **remedy** owed — with the obligation's status **derived** from those duty/module findings, never scored on its own. A **view over the duties**, not a new control layer.*

## Claim

A **named human institution answers** for this system, and people affected by consequential uses get **notice, meaningful review, a route to challenge, and remedy** — to the assurance depth stated below, no more.

## Scope & trigger

Consequential decisions or actions *involving* AI that materially affect a person — eligibility, ranking, screening, flagging, allocation, content removal, or a recommendation that drives such an outcome. Sits on the **consequential-event lifecycle**; the risk tier rises with irreversibility, essential-service status, and the vulnerability of those affected. Below the consequential threshold the obligation is a light screen, not a full case.

## Argument & evidence — the crosswalk

Each row routes the claim to an existing Charter Commitments duty (the *argument*) and the artifact that proves it (the *evidence*). Required depth is the minimum for a **high-stakes** tier; lower tiers relax it.

| Duty (argument) | What it must show for *answerability* | Evidence / module | Min. depth (high tier) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **D1 — accountable ownership** | a named institution + contact + governance that *answers* — never "the algorithm" | responsibility map | implementation-checked |
| **D6 — fairness, oversight, remedy** *(primary)* | meaningful human review/override; a reachable challenge route; remedy + outcome records | contestability module | **effectiveness-tested** |
| **D7 — transparency, claim-integrity** | notice that AI is materially involved; an understandable explanation of the decision | notice + explanation artefacts (duty-level) | evidence-observed |
| **D8 — lifecycle, incidents, withdrawal** | incidents and appeals logged; escalation and withdrawal routes that work | incident + appeal records | implementation-checked |
| **D4 — control, dependencies** *(supporting)* | who can restrict/recall/shut it down, and on what basis — no hidden powers | coercive-control / restriction-transparency module | evidence-observed |
| **D3 — risk register** *(supporting)* | the consequential-use risk is registered, tiered, and monitored | release risk assessment | documented |

## Remedy & escalation (derived)

The remedy *owed* is read from the impact lens, not invented here:

- **Individual** — correct → reconsider under human review → reverse/compensate; a published response time; the outcome recorded.
- **Collective / systemic** — repeated failures, disparate error rates, or group-level harm trigger **regulator-triggered or representative review**, not just individual appeals (a person cannot appeal a *pattern*).

## Failure / withdrawal triggers

The *claim is withdrawn* (not down-scored) when a supporting duty fails materially: no accountable owner; the challenge channel is shown to be theatre; a consequential use runs without the required human review; a restriction/shutdown power is hidden; or remedy records are curated. These resolve to duties 6 and 8.

## Derived status & claim discipline

The obligation is **not graded directly.** Its public status is the *weakest assurance depth across its primary rows* (D1, D6, D7, D8), with supporting rows noted. Public claims must track that depth:

- *Allowed* (when D6 is implementation-checked): "the challenge and human-review process was implementation-checked for this deployment."
- *Not allowed* (unless D6 is effectiveness-tested): "people have meaningful recourse."
- Any row at *not-assessed* is shown, never hidden.

## What this does not prove

Not legal compliance, not absence of harm, not fairness-of-outcome — only that the named controls were assessed to the stated depth, and that a route to challenge and remedy exists and was tested to that depth.

## What this prototype tested

- The four-block view **collapsed cleanly onto existing duties** — no new control was needed — which is the evidence that it's a *view*, not a fourth audit layer.
- It forced the part most frameworks dodge — **concrete remedy and the individual-vs-collective split** — which is why this obligation was prototyped first.
- Open question for the next ones: whether a shared **assurance-depth-by-tier table** lives here or in the certification model (likely the latter).
