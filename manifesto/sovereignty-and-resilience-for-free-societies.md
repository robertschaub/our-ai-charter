# AI Sovereignty and Democratic Resilience: Building Public AI as a Global Network

AI is becoming infrastructure: a layer through which people learn, work, govern, trade, receive public services, and form political judgement. When societies depend on such infrastructure, the central question is not only which model performs best this month. It is whether people, institutions, and democratic communities can understand, inspect, shape, and contest the systems they increasingly rely on.

That is why the momentum around open, public-interest, and sovereign AI matters. It is not a narrow technology story. It is a question of democratic resilience.

But an international cooperation agenda should not be organised around one country or one model. Switzerland has an important contribution to make through Apertus and the Swiss AI Initiative. Yet the deeper research points to a broader conclusion: the most credible path is a federated public-AI ecosystem, where several open or public-interest model efforts contribute different strengths.

So the short answer is: no, a strongly Swiss-centric framing is not justified for an international article. It is justified only if Switzerland is presented as one capable node in a wider network.

## Openness Is Not One Thing

The phrase "open AI" hides important differences. Some projects publish only model weights. Others publish training data, code, recipes, evaluations, intermediate artifacts, and documentation. Some are public-sector or nonprofit efforts. Others are commercial model releases with strong capability but weaker public accountability. Some are global in ambition; others are regionally grounded and therefore much better for local languages, public services, and cultural context.

For international cooperation, the question is not "which model should everyone adopt?" It is:

- Which models are open enough to inspect and improve?
- Which institutions are accountable enough to trust?
- Which communities are represented well enough to participate?
- Which infrastructure makes public access and shared evaluation possible?
- Which governance prevents a public initiative from being captured by a single state, company, funder, or host institution?

This changes the frame. Apertus is important, but it is not the center of the map. It is one strong public-AI node among several.

## A More International Map Of Strong Cooperation Nodes

**Apertus and the Swiss AI Initiative** are strong because they combine public research institutions, public supercomputing, multilingual ambition, and a fully open release posture. Apertus is presented as open weights, open data, open science, and documented methods. It is one of the clearest examples of a public-institutional model that others can inspect and build on.

**AI2 OLMo** is at least equally important for open-science credibility. OLMo's strength is the complete model flow: data, training code, recipes, evaluation tools, intermediate artifacts, and model releases. For an international network that needs reproducibility, auditability, and scientific challenge, OLMo is not a side reference. It is a benchmark for what serious openness can mean.

**OpenEuroLLM, EuroLLM, and ALIA** matter because they treat AI sovereignty as multilingual public infrastructure. OpenEuroLLM is building transparent European foundation models with data, documentation, training and testing code, and evaluation metrics. EuroLLM and Spain's ALIA show the practical direction: open multilingual models and language-technology infrastructure for public institutions, SMEs, researchers, and regional languages.

**SEA-LION** matters because public AI must not be English-first. Led from Singapore, SEA-LION is an open multilingual and multimodal model family built for Southeast Asian languages, cultures, and contexts. It also brings regional safety work, benchmarks, and community data efforts. That is exactly the kind of node an international public-AI network needs.

**LatamGPT** matters because regional representation is not optional. Its current public release is a Latin American model effort available on Hugging Face, based on Llama 3.1 70B with continued pretraining and supervised fine-tuning using regional data. Even where it depends on an existing base model, its cooperation value is clear: it brings Latin American data, institutions, languages, and use cases into the public-AI conversation.

**BharatGen** matters because India is building public-interest AI as a national multilingual, multimodal ecosystem: language, speech, vision, datasets, public-service use, skills, startups, and institutional capacity. Its public materials say only a subset of models, weights, recipes, and data will be released openly, so it should not be treated as fully open in the same sense as OLMo or Apertus. But as a public-capacity and language-sovereignty initiative, it is highly relevant.

**BLOOM and BigScience** remain important as a precedent. BLOOM is no longer the newest model, but it showed that large-scale multilingual model work can be organised as an international open-science collaboration involving hundreds of researchers.

**Public AI and Metagov** are not just background initiatives. They are part of the missing infrastructure layer. Public AI defines AI as public infrastructure: accessible, accountable, and producing permanent public goods. The Public AI Inference Utility is a practical access layer for public and sovereign models, and Metagov contributes governance and institutional design. This is the kind of connective tissue that can make cooperation real rather than rhetorical.

**Current AI, AI Alliance Project Tapestry, Common Corpus, and Common Pile** point to further building blocks: funding, open data, shared infrastructure, and federated model development. Tapestry is especially relevant because it asks how partners can co-train a shared base model while keeping local control over data and compute.

High-capability open-weight models such as **Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek, Falcon, and Llama** should also be part of the picture, but with a clear distinction. They can be excellent baselines for performance, deployment, adaptation, and benchmarking. Some have permissive licenses. But they are not automatically equivalent to public AI. If training data, recipes, governance, accountability, or license freedoms are incomplete, they should be treated as capability assets rather than full public-infrastructure models.

## What This Means For International Cooperation

The cooperation opportunity is not to crown one model. It is to connect complementary roles.

The network needs fully open reference models such as OLMo and Apertus. It needs regional and multilingual nodes such as SEA-LION, LatamGPT, BharatGen, ALIA, EuroLLM, and OpenEuroLLM. It needs high-capability open-weight baselines such as Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek, Falcon, and Llama. It needs access infrastructure such as the Public AI Inference Utility. It needs governance capacity from groups such as Public AI, Metagov, Current AI, AI Alliance, public research institutions, and standards-oriented evaluation communities. It needs lawful data commons such as Common Corpus and Common Pile. And it needs independent evaluation work from efforts such as MLCommons AILuminate and AI Verify.

This is a stronger international proposition than "support a Swiss model." It says: build a public-AI network where each node is evaluated by the role it can credibly play.

## A Practical Cooperation Agenda

The next phase should be concrete.

First, create a shared openness and governance map. Use frameworks such as the OSI Open Source AI Definition and the Model Openness Framework to distinguish fully open models, open-weight models, regional public models, and public-infrastructure projects. This would make cooperation more honest and reduce open-washing.

Second, build a public model and data registry. Each model should publish provenance, license terms, training-data documentation, known limitations, language coverage, evaluation results, release process, incident history, and routes for correction or contestation.

Third, federate public inference. Many societies will not train frontier models alone, but they can help operate, test, adapt, and govern public models. Public AI's Inference Utility is already a working example of how public and sovereign models can become usable beyond research labs.

Fourth, create shared evaluation protocols. Models should be compared not only by benchmark scores, but also by provenance, public-sector suitability, safety behaviour, reliability, correction processes, language coverage, and contestability. MLCommons AILuminate and AI Verify show that shared testing infrastructure is possible; public AI now needs a stronger evidence layer for whether deployed systems can show their work.

Fifth, build lawful multilingual data commons. Common Corpus and Common Pile show that open and licensed training data is becoming practical. Public AI needs more of this, especially for underrepresented languages and public-interest domains.

Sixth, establish co-stewardship rules before scale hardens power. Decision rules, funding sources, conflicts of interest, evaluation independence, incident reporting, and role separation should be public from the start.

Geneva 2027 can still be useful, but not as the center of the story. It can be one deadline and one convening window among several. The outcome should not be another declaration of principles. It should be a demonstrable cooperation package: a shared model registry, a public inference pilot, a multilingual evaluation protocol, and governance commitments for co-stewarded public-AI infrastructure.

## A Call For Co-Stewards

The next phase should be built by co-stewards: model builders, public research institutions, public-interest funders, compute providers, evaluators, public agencies, language communities, civil-society organisations, and governance experts.

The aim is not to replace commercial AI. It is to ensure that free societies are not dependent only on systems they cannot inspect, shape, or contest. Public AI should create a credible counterweight: open enough to study, capable enough to use, international enough to be legitimate, and governed well enough to deserve public trust.

Apertus can be one flagship node. OLMo can be another. OpenEuroLLM, SEA-LION, LatamGPT, BharatGen, ALIA, BLOOM, and other initiatives can each contribute different strengths. Public AI and Metagov can help connect access, governance, and movement-building. Current AI and AI Alliance can help with funding, coordination, and federated development.

Real digital sovereignty and resilience do not come from higher walls or national trophy projects. They come from shared public capacity: the ability to build, inspect, challenge, improve, and govern the infrastructure of the future together.

A network, not a national champion.

- Robert Schaub

## Publication Note: Are There Equally Strong Alternatives To Apertus?

Yes. Apertus is strong, but it is not uniquely strong for every cooperation role.

- **Strongest fully open / reproducibility peers:** OLMo, Apertus, OpenEuroLLM.
- **Strongest public or regional infrastructure peers:** Public AI, ALIA, SEA-LION, BharatGen, OpenEuroLLM, Apertus.
- **Strongest language and regional representation peers:** SEA-LION, LatamGPT, BharatGen, ALIA, EuroLLM/OpenEuroLLM, Apertus.
- **Strongest capability baselines:** Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek, Falcon, Llama.
- **Strongest data and cooperation infrastructure:** Common Corpus, Common Pile, Public AI Inference Utility, AI Alliance Project Tapestry.

The article should therefore frame Apertus as one leading node, not as the model around which international cooperation should revolve.
